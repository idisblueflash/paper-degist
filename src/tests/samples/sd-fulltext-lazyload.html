A systematic approach for developing a corpus of patient reported adverse drug events

PsyTAR corpus · Journal of Biomedical Informatics

Abstract

Patient reported adverse drug events remain underrepresented in the pharmacovigilance literature because the free text that patients write in online health forums is noisy, informal, and difficult to normalize against controlled vocabularies. In this work we describe a systematic, reproducible approach for constructing an annotated corpus of such reports so that downstream extraction models have a reliable gold standard to learn from and to be evaluated against.

Introduction

Spontaneous reporting systems capture only a small fraction of the adverse events that patients actually experience, and the events that are captured are filtered through clinicians rather than reported in the patient's own words. Social media and dedicated patient forums offer a complementary signal: patients describe symptoms, dosages, and timelines in narrative form, often before a formal report is ever filed. The challenge is that this narrative is unstructured. To turn it into evidence a corpus must be assembled with clear annotation guidelines, measured inter annotator agreement, and a schema that maps colloquial symptom language onto standard medical concepts.

Methods

We sampled posts from a large psychiatric medication forum and applied a two stage annotation protocol. In the first stage annotators marked spans denoting adverse drug reactions, withdrawal symptoms, signs and symptoms, and the drug indications themselves. In the second stage each marked span was normalized to a concept in a standard terminology, with disagreements resolved by adjudication. We measured agreement with Cohen's kappa at the span level and tracked the distribution of concept categories across the corpus. The annotation interface enforced the guideline so that every span carried a category label and, where applicable, a normalized concept identifier.

Results

The resulting corpus contains several thousand annotated posts spanning a broad set of medications and reaction types. Inter annotator agreement was substantial for the coarse categories and moderate for the fine grained normalization step, reflecting the genuine ambiguity of mapping lay language onto clinical concepts. Adverse drug reactions were the most frequent category, followed by signs and symptoms; withdrawal symptoms, though rarer, formed a distinct and clinically important cluster. A baseline sequence labeling model trained on the corpus recovered the majority of adverse reaction spans, demonstrating that the annotations are learnable and consistent.

Discussion

The corpus fills a gap between formal spontaneous reports and raw social media text by providing spans that are both grounded in patient narrative and normalized to a shared vocabulary. The moderate agreement on normalization is not a defect of the protocol but a faithful reflection of how imprecisely patients describe their experiences; it argues for models that predict a distribution over candidate concepts rather than a single label. We discuss threats to validity, including the single forum source and the psychiatric medication focus, and outline how the guideline generalizes to other therapeutic areas.

Conclusion

A carefully annotated corpus of patient reported adverse drug events is a prerequisite for trustworthy extraction from patient narratives. By publishing the guideline, the agreement measurements, and a baseline model we make the resource reproducible and extensible. Future work will broaden the drug coverage and integrate the normalized spans into a live pharmacovigilance signal detection pipeline.
